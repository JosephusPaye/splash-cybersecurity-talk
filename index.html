Cybersecurity Hygiene

10 practical tips to protect your organisation
from common cyber threats

A guide for non-profits and small teams

Why does this matter?

  • 43% of cyberattacks target small organisations — including non-profits
  • Non-profits hold sensitive data: donor info, financials, personal records
  • A single breach can destroy trust and funding
  • The good news: most attacks are preventable with basic hygiene
Cybersecurity hygiene = the ongoing routines, behaviours, and best practices that reduce vulnerabilities and mitigate risk.

1 Keep Software & Systems Up to Date

  • Outdated software has known security holes that attackers actively exploit
  • Vendors release patches specifically to fix these vulnerabilities
  • Enable automatic updates wherever possible — on computers, phones, and apps
Unpatched systems are the #1 way attackers get in. The WannaCry ransomware attack exploited a vulnerability that had a patch available for months.
Today: check that auto-updates are turned on for your OS, browser, and key apps.

2 Use Strong, Unique Passwords

  • Every account needs a distinct password — reusing passwords means one breach exposes everything
  • Aim for 12+ characters mixing uppercase, lowercase, numbers, and symbols
  • Use a password manager (e.g. Bitwarden, 1Password) to generate and store them securely
81% of data breaches involve weak or reused passwords. If your email password is the same as your donor database password, both are at risk.
Set up a free password manager like Bitwarden and start migrating your most important accounts.

3 Enable Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)

  • MFA adds a second layer of protection beyond your password
  • Typically a one-time code from an app, SMS, or biometric verification (fingerprint/face)
  • Even if someone steals your password, they still can't get in without the second factor
Without MFA, a stolen password = full account access. Phishing attacks regularly harvest passwords, but MFA stops them.
Enable MFA on your email, banking, and cloud storage accounts first — these are the highest-value targets.

4 Back Up Data Regularly

  • Backups protect against ransomware, hardware failure, and accidental deletion
  • Follow the 3-2-1 rule:
    • 3 copies of your data
    • 2 different storage types (e.g. local + cloud)
    • 1 copy offsite or in the cloud
  • Test your backups — a backup you can't restore is useless
Ransomware attacks encrypt your files and demand payment. Organisations with good backups can recover without paying.
Verify that your critical files (donor data, financials) are being backed up automatically today.

5 Train Staff to Spot Threats

  • People are the weakest link — and the strongest defence when trained well
  • Teach staff and volunteers to recognise phishing emails, suspicious links, and social engineering
  • Run simulated phishing campaigns to practise spotting fakes in a safe environment
91% of cyberattacks start with a phishing email. One click on a malicious link can compromise your entire organisation.
Schedule a short security awareness session for your team. Free resources are available from the ACSC (cyber.gov.au).

6 Limit Access Using Least Privilege

  • Give people access to only what they need for their role — nothing more
  • Review permissions quarterly, especially after staff or volunteer changes
  • Not everyone needs admin access — most people only need standard user accounts
If a volunteer account with full admin access gets compromised, the attacker has the keys to everything.
Audit who has admin access to your systems right now. Remove any access that isn't actively needed.

7 Monitor Activity & Audit Logs

  • Track login attempts, file changes, and admin actions across your systems
  • Set up alerts for unusual activity — logins outside business hours or from unfamiliar locations
  • Even simple monitoring catches problems early, before they become disasters
The average data breach goes undetected for 200+ days. The longer an attacker is in your systems, the more damage they do.
Enable login notifications on your email and cloud services. Review the "recent activity" logs in Google Workspace or Microsoft 365.

8 Secure Endpoints & Mobile Devices

  • Every device that connects to your network is a potential entry point — laptops, phones, tablets
  • Keep devices updated, encrypted, and protected with security software
  • Use mobile device management (MDM) if staff use personal devices for work
A lost or stolen phone without a lock screen could give someone access to your email, cloud files, and donor data.
Ensure all work devices have screen locks, encryption enabled, and remote wipe capability.

9 Decommission Old Devices & Accounts

  • Dormant accounts and old devices are forgotten backdoors into your systems
  • When someone leaves, immediately disable their accounts and revoke access
  • Wipe and securely dispose of old hardware — don't just throw it in a drawer
Former volunteers or staff with active accounts can still access sensitive data — intentionally or not.
Add account deprovisioning to your offboarding checklist. Review for any old accounts that should be disabled now.

10 Work With a Managed IT Partner

  • You don't have to do this alone — professional IT support provides ongoing monitoring and threat detection
  • A managed partner can tailor security to your organisation's specific needs and budget
  • Many offer non-profit pricing or discounted plans
Without dedicated expertise, threats evolve faster than your team can keep up. What worked last year may not protect you today.
Research managed IT providers in your area that offer non-profit rates. TechSoup is a good starting point.

Your Cybersecurity Checklist

  • Turn on automatic software updates
  • Set up a password manager
  • Enable MFA on critical accounts
  • Verify backups are running (3-2-1 rule)
  • Schedule a security awareness session
  • Audit admin access — remove what's not needed
  • Enable login alerts and review activity logs
  • Enforce screen locks and encryption on devices
  • Disable accounts for departed staff/volunteers
  • Explore managed IT support options

You don't need to be perfect.

You just need to be better than yesterday.

Start with one or two items from the checklist and build from there.
Every step you take makes your organisation a harder target.
